## WHAT THIS PROTOCOL IS AND WHY IT EXISTS

### The Problem It Solves

An enrollment is not a retained member. The moment an agent submits the enrollment and says goodbye, two things happen simultaneously: the agent counts it as a win, and the client is left alone with their decision for the first time.

That silence after the call is where disenrollments are born.

The client who said yes under the momentum of a well-run call may not feel the same way ten minutes later when their spouse asks what they did, or their daughter calls, or they simply sit with it and the doubt creeps in. A client who hangs up uncertain will disenroll. It is not a question of if — it is a question of when.

The Close Confirmation Protocol exists for one reason: **to ensure the client hangs up certain.**

Certain they made the right decision. Certain the plan is better. Certain enough to stay enrolled when someone questions them about it — and someone will.

### What It Is Not

This protocol is not a second close. The enrollment is already submitted. This is not an attempt to re-sell the plan or revisit the comparison. It is a 20–30 second re-anchoring of a decision that has already been made — delivered in a way that makes the client feel like they won, not like they survived a sales call.

It is not compliance language. It does not replace the required script. It is delivered after all compliance steps are complete and the enrollment is confirmed in the system.

---

## THE STRUCTURE

The Close Confirmation Protocol has three components, delivered in sequence. Total time: 20–30 seconds.

---

### Component 1 — The Anchor Statement
**Purpose:** Remind the client of the specific reason they said yes — using their own words or their own situation where possible.

This is not a generic "you made a great choice." It is specific to this client. It references something real from the call — a benefit they were excited about, a concern that was resolved, a number that landed.

**Structure:**
*"[Client name], you're all set. Remember what we talked about — [reference the specific thing that mattered to them]."*

**For a clean close — client said yes confidently:**
- *"Mrs. Johnson, you're all set. Remember — that $1,200 dental benefit kicks in January 1st. That appointment you've been putting off? That's covered."*
- *"Mr. Williams, you're all set. Your doctor is in-network, your medications are covered, and you've got $0 premium. Everything you asked about — it's all there."*
- *"Ms. Davis, you're all set. You're going from $47 a month down to zero, and you're picking up that grocery card. That's $564 back in your pocket this year."*

**For a difficult close — client said yes slowly, with hesitation:**

Do not open with "you made a smart move." A client who said yes reluctantly does not need validation — they need their fear resolved one final time before you hang up. Acknowledge what they were worried about, then close the loop on it.

- *"I know you had some hesitation — and I want you to feel good about this before we hang up. Your doctor [name] is confirmed in that plan. That was your concern, and it's resolved. You did the right thing."*
- *"I know this felt like a big decision. Here's what I want you to remember: you're not losing anything you have. You're adding to it. The [benefit] starts [date]. That's yours now."*
- *"I could hear you thinking it through — and you made the right call. The plan you just left had [gap]. That's gone now. Starting [date], you're covered the right way."*

---

### Component 2 — The Confidence Statement
**Purpose:** Give the client language they can use when someone questions their decision — because someone will.

Most disenrollments are not driven by the client's own doubt. They are driven by a family member, a friend, or a neighbor who hears about the switch and raises concerns the client doesn't know how to answer. If the client cannot explain confidently why they made the change, they will defer to the doubter. The Confidence Statement gives them the words before that conversation happens.

**Structure:**
*"If anyone asks you why you switched, here's exactly what you can tell them: [simple, clear, one-sentence explanation of the value]."*

**By call type — because who will question them depends on who they are:**

*Money Caller — spouse or adult child will ask "why did you change your plan?"*
> *"If your family asks, you can tell them: 'I had a licensed agent run the numbers. I was leaving $[amount] a year on the table with my old plan. My doctor is still covered and I'm getting [benefit] I wasn't getting before.'"*

*Scared Switcher — neighbor or friend will say "I heard you should never change your Medicare plan."*
> *"If anyone tells you that you shouldn't have switched, you can tell them: 'I verified my doctor is still in-network and my medications are still covered. Everything I had, I kept — and now I also get [benefit].'"*

*Brand Loyalist — will second-guess themselves when they see their old carrier's name.*
> *"If you start second-guessing the carrier — remember, we ran the comparison inside [old carrier] first. This was the stronger plan. Same network, more benefit. You didn't leave [carrier] behind; you upgraded."*

*Veteran — adult child or fellow veteran will question why they "added" a plan.*
> *"If someone asks why you added Medicare to your VA — you can tell them: 'My VA covers everything at the VA. This covers everything outside it. They work together. I'm not replacing anything. I just stopped having a gap.'"*

*Third Party Controlled — the family member who wasn't on the call will ask.*
> *"When [daughter/son] asks about this — and they will — here's what you can tell them: 'A licensed agent ran the side-by-side comparison with me. My doctor is confirmed in-network. My medications are covered. And I'm getting [benefit] I wasn't getting before. They can call the agent directly if they want to go through it.'"*

---

### Component 3 — The Forward Close
**Purpose:** End the call with the client looking forward, not looking back. Give them something to anticipate so the last thing they feel is anticipation, not anxiety.

**Structure:**
*"You'll get your new card in the mail in [timeframe]. Your coverage starts [date]. You made a smart move today, [client name]. Enjoy [something specific they mentioned — the dental, the card, the savings]."*

**Examples:**
- *"Your new card will arrive in 7–10 business days. Coverage starts January 1st. You made a smart move today, Mrs. Johnson. Go ahead and schedule that dental appointment."*
- *"Everything kicks in on the first. You'll get confirmation in the mail this week. You did the right thing today, Mr. Williams — your coverage is solid."*
- *"Your card comes in about two weeks. January 1st you're all set. Well done today, Ms. Davis — that grocery card is going to make a real difference."*

---

## THE TEST

Before you hang up, the client should be able to answer these three questions on their own — without you prompting them:

1. **What plan did I just enroll in and when does it start?**
2. **Why did I switch?**
3. **What is the one thing I'm most looking forward to?**

If they cannot answer all three, the Close Confirmation was not executed effectively. The Anchor Statement covers 1. The Confidence Statement covers 2. The Forward Close covers 3. All three components are required. Skipping any one of them leaves a gap the client will fill with doubt.

---

**Example 2 — Scared Switcher, difficult close (client hesitated, eventually said yes):**

*"Ms. Davis, I know you had to think about this — and I want you to feel good before we hang up. Dr. [name] is confirmed in this plan. That was your concern, and it's resolved.*

*If anyone asks you about the switch, you can tell them: 'I verified my doctor is still covered. Everything I had, I kept — and I'm picking up dental and a grocery card I didn't have before.'*

*Your card arrives in about two weeks. Coverage starts the first. You did the right thing today — go enjoy that grocery card."*

---

**Example 3 — Veteran, clean close (VA fear was neutralized earlier in the call):**

*"Mr. Thompson, you're all set. Remember — your VA care doesn't change. What this plan does is cover everything outside the VA, so you're not exposed anymore. The dental, the OTC card, the emergency coverage — that's all yours now starting [date].*

*If anyone — family, fellow vets — asks why you added Medicare, you can tell them: 'My VA covers the VA. This covers everything else. They work together. I just stopped having a gap.'*

*You'll get your card in about 7 to 10 days. You made a smart move today."*

---

**Example 4 — Third Party Controlled, adult child was on the call:**

*"Mrs. Garcia, you're all set — and [daughter's name], I'm glad you were on this call. Mrs. Garcia, remember: Dr. [name] is confirmed in-network, your medications are covered, and you're picking up [benefit] starting [date].*

*[Daughter's name] — if you have any questions after you've had a chance to look things over, my direct number is [number]. Call me anytime.*

*Mrs. Garcia, your card comes in about 7 to 10 days. You made the right call today."*

## HOW THIS IS TRAINED

**In Kajabi:** The concept and purpose are taught in Submodule 5. Agents learn what the Hollow Yes costs before they learn the fix. The protocol structure is introduced as the direct solution to Pattern 9.

**In Live Training:** One evergreen session is dedicated to the Close Confirmation. Agents roleplay it in pairs — including the difficult close version. Every agent delivers the full protocol at least twice before the session ends. It must become automatic — not something they think about after enrollment, but something that happens as naturally as the compliance script.

**In QA Review:** The Gemini QA Gem audits every enrolled call for Close Confirmation execution. The report notes whether it was executed, partially executed, or absent — and flags absent executions as Hollow Yes risk.

**Standard:** Every enrolled call ends with Close Confirmation. No exceptions. A submitted enrollment without a Close Confirmation is an incomplete call.

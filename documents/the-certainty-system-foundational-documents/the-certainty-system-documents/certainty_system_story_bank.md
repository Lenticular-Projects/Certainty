# THE CERTAINTY SYSTEM
## Story Bank — Agent Reference
### 60-Second Client Stories | Memorize These. Use Them Live.

---

## WHAT THIS IS — AND HOW TO USE IT

A story is not a rebuttal. A rebuttal pushes back. A story walks around the wall.

When a client is resistant, defensive, or drifting — when they say something that sounds like a no but probably isn't — the instinct is to counter it with logic or product information. That almost never works. What works is dropping the temperature. Showing them that someone exactly like them had the exact same thought, the exact same hesitation — and found out it wasn't the obstacle they thought it was.

That's what these stories do. They don't argue. They don't correct. They level with the client, person to person, and give them a way to change their mind without losing face.

**When to reach for a story:**
- The client is on the fence — not a hard no, but not moving forward
- They've raised an objection and a straight rebuttal would feel like an argument
- The call has drifted into doubt or hesitation and you need to bring it back without pressure
- You've made the case logically and it hasn't landed — try it emotionally instead

**When NOT to use a story:**
- The client is GREEN and ready — don't slow down a moving call with a story. Close.
- The client has a hard, logical barrier (doctor out of network, medication not covered) — a story won't fix a real problem. Solve it.
- You're using it as a filler — if you reach for a story because you don't know what to say next, the client will feel it. Know *why* you're telling it before you start.

---

## THE 4-PART STRUCTURE

Every story is built on four parts, delivered in 60 seconds or less. You don't say it word for word every time — you know it well enough to drop it naturally, mid-call, without breaking stride.

1. **Someone like you** — establish that the story subject shares this client's exact situation, concern, or hesitation. The more specific, the better. *"Fixed income, same zip code, been on the same plan for two years."*
2. **Had the same situation** — name the specific fear or friction point. Not generic. The thing *this client* just said.
3. **Made this move** — the decision to look or enroll. Keep it simple. One sentence.
4. **Here's what changed** — one concrete outcome connected to something *this specific client* has already told you they care about. This is where the story closes. If you don't connect it to their words, it's just an anecdote.

**The rule:** Part 4 has to land on something the client said earlier in the call. If you can't connect it to their specific situation, you're not ready to tell the story yet — ask one more discovery question first.

## TYPE 1 — THE MONEY CALLER
*They called about a specific benefit. Grocery card, OTC allowance, Part B giveback. They are motivated. The danger isn't resistance — it's mismatched expectations. A lot of these callers have seen a commercial promising $1,200 or $3,000 a month. When the real number is $148 or $220, they feel let down before you've said anything useful. The story's job, sometimes right at the top of the call, is to reset that frame before the disappointment becomes a wall.*

**Note on timing:** For most call types, you deploy a story in response to something the client says. For Type 1, you sometimes need to front-load it — drop the expectation reset in the first 60 seconds, before the client's commercial number poisons the well. If you hear any version of "I'm calling about the card" and their tone is already impatient or clipped, don't wait for the objection. Set the frame first.

---

**Variation A — Motivated and ready, no commercial expectation in the way**
*Trigger: "I'm calling about the card" / "Someone said I could get grocery money" / "I heard I could get money back on my Social Security" — and their tone is open, not agitated*

*"I had a woman call in not long ago — similar situation to yours, fixed income, really just trying to stretch every dollar. She was on a plan she'd been on for two years and thought it was fine. When I pulled up what was actually available in her area, she was leaving $255 a month on the table — $3,000 a year — in grocery and utility benefit she didn't even know existed. She went quiet for a second, then she said, 'Why didn't anyone tell me this?' She enrolled that day. The card came in the mail ten days later."*

**Why this works:** She wasn't gaining something new — she was *reclaiming* money that was already hers. That's the frame. Use when the caller is open and motivated — don't use it when they're already agitated about a commercial number. Read the tone first.

---

**Variation B — They came in expecting a big number. Reset the frame early.**
*Trigger: Any commercial reference in the first 30 seconds — "I'm calling about the $1,200" / "The TV said I'd get $900 a month" / impatient tone right from the start*

*Use this one early — before the expectation gap turns into frustration.*

*"Before I pull up your information, I want to give you a quick heads up about something — because I'd rather be straight with you now than have you feel misled later. Those commercials you see advertise the highest benefit available anywhere in the country. Your actual benefit depends on your zip code and what plans are in your area. I've got people who call in expecting $3,000 and end up with $180 — and I've had people who were ready to hang up over that, until I showed them what the full picture looked like. That $180 a month is $2,160 a year. And when I add in what they were paying in premiums and deductibles on their old plan, the real swing was over $4,000. So before you decide anything, let me pull up what's actually available for you. Fair enough?"*

**Why this works:** Gets ahead of the disappointment instead of reacting to it. Positions you as honest, not defensive. Gives the real number meaning before they've decided it's not enough. The math in the story does the recontextualization — monthly feels small, annual feels real, total swing is the close.

---

**Variation C — They saw the number, it feels too good to be true**
*Trigger: "That sounds like a lot" / "I don't understand why they'd just give me that" / "Is this legitimate?"*

*"I hear that reaction all the time — and I'll tell you what I told a gentleman last month when he said the same thing. He asked me, 'Why would they give me $220 a month for groceries?' I told him the truth: the government funds these benefits through Medicare. The insurance company distributes them. The reason most people don't know about it is because nobody calls to tell you. He said, 'So this money is just sitting there?' I said, 'Every month you don't have this plan, yes.' He enrolled. Said he felt like he'd been leaving a check uncashed for two years."*

**Why this works:** Reframes skepticism as understandable — then recontextualizes inaction as the real loss.

---

**Variation D — The specific benefit they called about isn't available in their area**
*Trigger: "But I called specifically for the giveback / the $300 card" / "If I can't get that, I'm not interested" / caller fixated on one benefit that doesn't exist in their zip code*

*"I completely understand — and I want to show you something before we hang up, because I see this all the time. I had a woman who called specifically about the Part B giveback. Her zip code didn't have it. She was ready to hang up. Before she did, I pulled up what was actually in her area. No giveback — but there was a $195 monthly grocery card and a $0 premium. She'd been paying $23 a month. That's $2,574 a year she almost walked away from because the one thing she called about wasn't there. Give me 60 seconds before we close this out."*

**Why this works:** Keeps the motivated caller on the line when the specific benefit isn't available. Pivots from the single benefit they fixated on to the total value that's actually there.

## TYPE 2 — THE SCARED SWITCHER
*Interested but afraid of losing something — their doctor, their current plan, their carrier, their "insurance lady." Fear is the objection underneath every word they say. The story has to name the fear before it redirects.*

---

**Variation A — "I don't want to change / I've been on this plan for years"**
*Trigger: "I'm happy with what I have" / "My plan works for me" / "The devil you know"*

*"I get that all the time — and I said the same thing to a gentleman last month who told me he'd been on his plan for three years and wasn't touching it. I told him I wasn't asking him to touch anything. Just let me look. Sixty seconds later I showed him his plan had an $11 monthly premium he didn't know he was paying, a $615 drug deductible, and a $9,250 out-of-pocket max if something serious happened. The plan I found him had $0 across all three. Same network. Same area. His exact doctors, covered. He said, 'I had no idea.' He switched. His quality of life went up and his risk went down — on the same day."*

**Why this works:** Reframes the switch from a risk into the safer choice. Staying was the gamble.

---

**Variation B — "I'm worried about losing my doctor"**
*Trigger: "I need to make sure my doctor is covered" / "I've had my cardiologist for 10 years, I'm not switching" / "My doctor is the most important thing"*

*"That's the first thing I check on every call — before I show anyone anything. I had a woman last week, same concern, been seeing her oncologist for four years. She told me that was a dealbreaker and I told her good, then let's start there. I pulled her doctor up in our system live while she was on the phone. In-network. Same office. Zero change to her specialist relationship. Then I showed her what the plan offered on top of that — $185 in monthly grocery benefit she wasn't getting. She said, 'So I keep my doctor and I get the card?' I said yes. She enrolled in under ten minutes."*

**Why this works:** Makes doctor verification the first move, not an afterthought. Removes the fear before presenting anything else.

---

**Variation C — "I love my carrier / I don't want to leave [carrier name]"**
*Trigger: "I've been with Humana/UHC/Aetna for years" / "I trust my carrier" / "I'm loyal to them"*

*"I hear you — and that loyalty makes complete sense if they've taken care of you. I had a woman who told me the same thing about her carrier. She'd been with them eight years and wasn't interested in hearing about anything else. I didn't ask her to leave them. I asked her if she knew what tier her current plan was within that same carrier. She didn't. Turns out there was a higher-tier plan under the same carrier in her area with a $220 grocery card, $0 drug deductible, and her same doctors. She didn't switch carriers. She upgraded. She didn't understand why no one had told her about the better plan sooner."*

**Why this works:** Removes the binary. You're not asking them to leave — you're asking them to upgrade. Reframes brand loyalty as compatible with the move.

## TYPE 3 — THE MISINFORMED CALLER
*Operating on false information from a source they trust — a doctor's office, a family member, a previous agent. The resistance isn't stubbornness. It's loyalty to bad information. The story makes the agent the authority without attacking the source.*

---

**Variation A — Doctor's office gave wrong information**
*Trigger: "My doctor's office said I can't switch right now" / "They told me to wait until open enrollment"*

*"I had a gentleman call in a few weeks ago — his doctor's office had told him he couldn't switch plans because of an upcoming procedure. He'd been sitting on that for two months. I pulled up his situation live while we talked. That restriction didn't exist. His procedure was fully covered under the new plan, same facility, same doctor. He'd been waiting two months and losing benefit money every month for something that wasn't even true. We enrolled him before we hung up."*

**Why this works:** Uses live system access as authority without saying the doctor's office was wrong — just that the situation was different than what they thought.

---

**Variation B — Family member or previous agent gave wrong information**
*Trigger: "My son looked this up and said it wasn't worth it" / "An agent told me last year I didn't qualify" / "I heard these plans have bad networks"*

*"You know what, I appreciate that someone was looking out for you — that's the right instinct. I had a woman whose daughter had researched her Medicare options and told her she was already on the best plan available. I asked if her daughter was a licensed agent who works with all carriers in her area. She wasn't. I pulled up the live options. There was a plan with $240 monthly grocery benefit and $0 out-of-pocket max that hadn't existed last year. Her daughter was working off old information from a Google search. The woman said, 'I'm going to have to tell her she was wrong.' We enrolled her that day."*

**Why this works:** Respects the source while repositioning the agent as the live, credentialed authority. The story does the work of dismantling the false belief without the agent directly contradicting anyone.

## TYPE 4 — THE THIRD PARTY CONTROLLED
*The decision sits with someone not on the call — or someone who just joined the call and is skeptical. The story reframes the third party from an obstacle into an ally.*

---

**Variation A — Client wants to talk to family before deciding**
*Trigger: "I need to talk to my son/daughter/husband first" / "I don't make decisions like this without my wife"*

*"I spoke with a man recently who was on the fence — wanted to talk to his brother first. I actually suggested we get the brother on the line right then. Good thing we did, because his brother was the one who said, 'Why would you not do this? You called them about the card and they found it.' The brother became the reason he enrolled, not the reason he didn't. When you include the person they trust, they almost always become your ally."*

**Why this works:** Reframes the delay as unnecessary — and shows the third party becoming a closer, not a blocker.

---

**Variation B — Third party is on the call and skeptical**
*Trigger: Family member joins mid-call and starts asking hard questions or expressing doubt*

*"I had a call last month where a woman's daughter got on the line about halfway through — sharp, skeptical, exactly the right questions. She asked me how I got paid, whether this would affect her mother's coverage, and why this plan was better than what she had. I walked her through all of it. By the end of the call, the daughter was the one who said, 'Mom, I think you should do this.' The people who ask the hardest questions are usually the best advocates once they get the real answers. I'd rather have them on the call than have them in the background after we hang up."*

**Why this works:** Reframes the skeptical family member as an asset, not a threat. Signals confidence to both parties.

## TYPE 5 — THE DETAIL STALLER
*"I want to think about it" is almost never about thinking. It's a polite exit from a call the agent stopped leading. The story makes inaction expensive before the client leaves.*

---

**Variation A — "I want to think about it"**
*Trigger: "I'm not ready to make a decision today" / "Let me sit with this" / "Can you call me back next week"*

*"I had a woman last month — sharp, asked great questions, said she just wanted to look it up herself before she committed. I completely understood. And then I showed her one thing before she went. Every month she waited was $210 in grocery benefit she couldn't get back. Not going forward — gone. She sat with that for about five seconds and said, 'Well when would it start?' We enrolled her right there. Three weeks later she called to tell me the card came."*

**Why this works:** Recontextualizes the cost of waiting in exact dollars. Makes the delay feel expensive before they exit.

---

**Variation B — "Send me something / Give me the plan number so I can research it"**
*Trigger: "What's the plan ID?" / "Can you email me the details?" / "I want to look this up online"*

*"I had a gentleman who asked me for the plan number so he could research it himself — which I completely understand. Before I gave it to him, I told him something honest: everything he'd find online would be the same information I'm looking at right now, except I also have his specific doctor confirmed as in-network, his zip code benefit pulled, and his current plan comparison already done. I asked him what he was hoping to find that we hadn't already covered. He thought about it. Said he didn't know. We finished the enrollment in eight minutes."*

**Why this works:** Names what they're really doing — exiting — without making them feel attacked. Removes the rationale for leaving by showing the research is already done.

## TYPE 6 — THE TIME BOMB
*Willing and interested, but the clock is running. A ride arriving. Exhaustion. Pain. Another appointment. The agent who treats this like a normal call loses it. Speed and competence are the rapport.*

---

**Variation A — Hard time constraint (ride coming, appointment)**
*Trigger: "I only have a few minutes" / "My ride is coming" / "I have an appointment at 2"*

*"I had a woman call in — her ride was coming in 20 minutes to take her to an appointment. She was ready, she just didn't think we had enough time. I told her we had plenty of time, I just needed one thing — her doctor's name. Sixty seconds to confirm coverage, two minutes to walk her through the benefit, enrolled with eight minutes to spare. She called me from the parking lot afterward. Said she couldn't believe how fast it was. When someone tells you they're short on time, that's not a reason to stop. That's a reason to move."*

**Why this works:** Models exactly how fast the enrollment can happen. Removes the time objection by showing the timeline in advance.

---

**Variation B — Physical exhaustion or health limitation**
*Trigger: "I'm not feeling well" / "I'm tired" / "Can we do this another time when I'm feeling better"*

*"I had a gentleman on a call last month — he told me about ten minutes in that he was in pain and couldn't talk much longer. I told him that was fine and I only needed two more minutes from him. I already had his doctor confirmed, his benefit number pulled, and his current plan in front of me. The hard work was done. We finished the enrollment in under three minutes. He thanked me for not wasting his time. When someone's not feeling well, the kindest thing you can do is be fast and competent — not schedule a callback."*

**Why this works:** Reframes speed as respect, not pressure. Shows the enrollment can happen even in difficult conditions.

## TYPE 7 — THE COMMERCIAL MYTH CALLER
*They called because a TV commercial promised a number that doesn't match their zip code. They're not resistant — they're confused and feel deceived. The story joins their frustration before redirecting to what's real.*

---

**Variation A — Angry about the gap between the commercial and the offer**
*Trigger: "The commercial said $900 / $6,000 / $1,200 — why are you showing me less?" / "I feel like I was misled"*

*"I get that frustration every single day — and honestly, you're right to feel it. I had a gentleman who called furious. The commercial said $900 a month and I was showing him $148. He felt like someone had lied to him. I told him someone had — those commercials show the highest benefit available anywhere in the country. They're not wrong about the benefit existing. They're just not telling you it's zip code specific. Then I pulled up what was actually available for him. It wasn't $900. It was $148 a month — $1,776 a year — in grocery benefit he wasn't getting at all on his current plan. He went quiet. Then he said, 'OK, that's still real money.' He enrolled."*

**Why this works:** Validates the frustration, explains the commercial without defending it, then pivots immediately to the real available value.

---

**Variation B — Suspicious and guarded, not just frustrated**
*Trigger: "How do I know this is real?" / "This sounds like a scam" / "I've heard this before and it wasn't what they said"*

*"I had a woman call in last month who'd been burned before — called a number from a commercial, gave her information, and ended up with a plan that didn't cover her doctor. She was ready to hang up the moment I answered. I told her I understood exactly why she felt that way, and then I asked her to let me do one thing before she made up her mind: I looked up her doctor live, on the phone, while she listened. Confirmed in network. Then I pulled her benefit. Then I gave her the plan number and told her she could verify everything we discussed on Medicare.gov before she agreed to anything. She said, 'You're the first person who ever offered to let me check your work.' We enrolled her."*

**Why this works:** Leads with transparency. Gives the skeptical client control — which is the only thing that disarms deep distrust.

## TYPE 8 — THE VETERAN
*Uses VA benefits and believes Medicare Advantage competes with or threatens them. The fear is displacement — losing what they earned. The story neutralizes that fear before anything else is said.*

---

**Variation A — "I don't want to mess with my VA benefits"**
*Trigger: "I use the VA for everything" / "I earned these benefits, I'm not risking them" / "My VA doctor is my doctor"*

*"I talk to veterans about this all the time — and the concern makes complete sense if nobody's ever explained how these two things actually work together. I had a gentleman, 22 years Army, used the VA for everything, didn't want to touch it. Said he'd earned those benefits and wasn't going to risk them for an insurance card. I told him he wasn't risking anything. His VA covers everything at the VA. This plan covers everything outside the VA — the specialist his VA doesn't have, dental, vision, the ER three states away when he's visiting his daughter. They don't compete. They stack. He asked me why no one had ever explained it that way. We enrolled him that day."*

**Why this works:** Recontextualizes the two systems as additive. The VA stays. This adds to it. Neutralize the displacement fear before mentioning anything else.

---

**Variation B — "I had a bad experience with Medicare before"**
*Trigger: Veteran who previously enrolled and had a problem — doctor issue, billing issue, felt like the VA was affected*

*"I hear you — and if something went wrong before, that's on whoever handled your enrollment. I had a veteran call in who'd been burned by a previous plan that had listed his VA specialist as in-network and then billed him anyway. He was done with Medicare Advantage entirely. I told him his instinct was right — that plan was wrong for him. Then I asked him one question: did they check whether his VA care was affected by the enrollment, or just whether the doctor was listed? They hadn't checked. We went through his VA coverage line by line. Nothing would have touched it. The problem wasn't Medicare Advantage — it was the wrong plan, chosen without the right verification. He enrolled into a plan we verified together."*

**Why this works:** Validates the bad experience without defending the industry. Repositions the agent as the person who does it right — and demonstrates what right looks like.

## TYPE 9 — THE TIMING OBJECTOR
*Willing and interested but fixated on a timing issue that sounds logical and is almost always a stall. The story makes the wait feel expensive — in specific dollars — before they exit.*

---

**Variation A — "I'm waiting for Medicaid / waiting for something to come through"**
*Trigger: "I'm waiting to see if I qualify for Medicaid" / "I'm waiting on my disability" / "I want to wait until my situation is settled"*

*"I had a woman who told me she was waiting for Medicaid to come through before she made any changes. I asked her when she'd applied. She said four months ago. I asked what Medicaid had told her. She said they told her she didn't qualify. She was waiting for something that had already said no. Every month she waited was $210 in grocery benefit and a $0 premium plan she was paying $23 a month for instead. I walked her through that number. She went quiet. Said, 'I didn't realize I was paying for waiting.' We enrolled her before the call ended."*

**Why this works:** Exposes the stall as a stall — gently, using their own timeline. Makes the cost of waiting concrete and specific.

---

**Variation B — "I have a procedure coming up / I don't want to change anything right now"**
*Trigger: "I'm having surgery next month" / "I have an appointment next week, I don't want to mess anything up" / "Let me get through this and then call me back"*

*"I had a gentleman who told me he had a procedure scheduled in three weeks and didn't want to change anything before it. I completely understood. Then I asked him one question: had he verified his surgeon was covered under his current plan? He hadn't. We pulled it up together. His surgeon wasn't in-network on his current plan — but was in-network on the new one. He would have walked into that procedure with unexpected out-of-pocket costs he didn't know about. We enrolled him that day. His exact surgeon, covered. His procedure, three weeks later, without a billing surprise."*

**Why this works:** Reframes the timing concern as the reason to act now, not later. Uses the client's own upcoming event as the urgency.

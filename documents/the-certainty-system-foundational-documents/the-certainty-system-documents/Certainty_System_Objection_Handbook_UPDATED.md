### Medicare Advantage Sales | Live-Call Reference

> **How to use this handbook:** When a client says something that stops the call, find it here. Every entry is organized by what the client said. Look up the words you just heard. Deploy the response.

## QUICK REFERENCE: THE FRAMEWORK

**Three Client Signals**
- 🟢 **GREEN — Open.** Engaged, asking questions, leaning in. Present value, build the math, execute The Math Breakdown.
- 🔴 **RED — Resistant.** Pushing back, going quiet, stalling. Name the emotion first. Never respond with logic.
- 🟡 **YELLOW — Drifting.** Tangent, dead air, gone social. Validate in one sentence. Bridge back with a forward statement.

**Rule: Always handle RED before YELLOW.**

**Four Pillars**
- **Pillar 1 — Persuasion.** Lead with authority. Assumptive frame. Never ask permission.
- **Pillar 2 — Reframing.** Name the emotion. Redirect the meaning.
- **Pillar 3 — The Shift / The Math Breakdown.** Change what they're comparing against. Show them what they're losing, not what they'd gain.
- **Pillar 4 — Refocusing.** Validate. Bridge back. Narrate navigation to prevent dead air.

**Loss Aversion Principle:** People feel losses twice as powerfully as gains. Every reframe must be about what they're currently giving away — not what they would gain.

**The Mission:** Improve the client's quality of life using benefits that already exist and are already funded. Most clients pay $0 premium. This is not about getting something new — it is about not leaving behind what is already theirs.

**One-Call-Close Principle:** Every call is a same-day enrollment call. There is no appointment to set, no second chance to close what you can close right now. Urgency is not anxiety — it is knowing that every day a client stays on the wrong plan is money they are giving away. That is real. Lead from that truth.

**Enrollment Safety Net Language (approved):** *"If you enroll today and something isn't right, call me directly. I will work to find a resolution through the proper enrollment channels. That's what I'm here for."*

**Medicare Advantage Enrollment Accuracy:**
- There is no unconditional cancellation window for Medicare Advantage.
- **OEP (Open Enrollment Period):** January 1 – March 31 allows one plan change.
- **SEP (Special Enrollment Period):** Qualifying life events (move to new service area, loss of Medicaid, etc.).
- Do not promise enrollment reversal — use the safety net language above.

## THE MATH BREAKDOWN — HOW TO EXECUTE IT

*Referenced throughout this handbook. Every agent must know all three steps before getting on a call.*

The Math Breakdown is not a script. It is a three-step sequence that transforms a monthly benefit number into a felt, real, personal loss — so that staying on the wrong plan becomes more uncomfortable than changing it.

**Step 1 — State the comparison.**
Side by side. Current benefit vs. available benefit. Specific numbers. No ambiguity.
> *"Right now you're getting $[X] per month. What I'm looking at for your area gives you $[Y] per month."*

**Step 2 — Annualize it.**
Always multiply by 12. Monthly numbers feel trivial. Annual numbers land.
> *"That's $[monthly difference] a month — which is $[annual difference] a year."*

| Timeframe | Current Plan | Available Plan | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly benefit | $[amount] | $[amount] | +$[amount]/mo — client gains this every month |
| Annual benefit | $[amount] | $[amount] | +$[amount]/yr — this is the real number |
| Copay/cost difference (est.) | $[amount]/visit | $[amount]/visit | -$[amount]/visit — saves on each use |
| **Annual Net Impact** | $[total] | $[total] | **Show the math: "$X more in benefits minus $Y in any added costs = $Z net gain per year"** |

*Label every row with its timeframe. Never mix monthly and annual numbers without flagging them. The bottom line must show the arithmetic written out in plain English — not just a final number.*

**Step 3 — Humanize the cost of inaction.**
Connect the annual number to something real *this specific client* told you during the call. This is the close. Steps 1 and 2 are setup — Step 3 is where the enrollment happens.

Use their own words. Examples of Step 3 executed correctly:
- Client mentioned utilities and groceries → *"That $255 a month — $3,060 a year — that's your utility bills and your grocery cart. Right now that money is just sitting there."*
- Client said she couldn't drive anymore → *"That $128 coming back into your Social Security every month — that's your transportation money. You don't have to depend on anyone for a ride."*
- Client mentioned only $100 left after the light bill → *"That $127 coming back covers your light bill with money left over."*
- Client was denied Medicaid by $7 → *"The state said no over $7. This plan says yes to $420 a year in groceries. Those aren't the same conversation."*

**Step 3 only works if you were listening during discovery.** Every detail the client shares — finances, health, family, frustrations — is potential close material. Hold it. Deploy it when The Math Breakdown lands.

**The Waiting Tax Frame** (use when client stalls after The Math Breakdown):
> *"Every month you stay where you are, $[amount] that belongs to you goes unclaimed. That's not staying safe — that's choosing to leave it behind."*

**Incomplete Pivot = Lost Enrollment.** The most common failure is stopping at Step 2. The agent states the annual number, the client says "I need to think about it," and the call ends. Step 3 is what removes the exit. If you skip it, you are leaving the close on the table.

# SECTION 1: FEAR OF CHANGE & SWITCHING OBJECTIONS

---

## "I don't want to change my plan."
*Variations: "I like what I have." / "If it ain't broke, don't fix it." / "I'd rather leave well enough alone." / "I just want to stick with what I have right now."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — fear of the unknown, inertia

**What's underneath:** This client isn't saying no to a better plan. They're saying no to the risk of losing something that feels safe. The word "change" activates loss aversion immediately.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"Change is sometimes a good thing!"* — invalidates their experience
- ❌ *"This plan is actually better."* — logic against emotion; they will dig in and defend what they have
- ❌ *"OK, that's fine. Don't worry about it."* — total capitulation; call ends, opportunity gone
- ❌ *"Did I not help you find a plan that has stronger benefits?"* — puts the client in a position to say no

**Correct first response:**
> *"I completely understand — and I wouldn't want you to change anything either. But before I let you go, I want to make sure what you have is still the right plan for your situation this year. Plans restructure their benefits every January — what was the best option last year isn't always the best option now. It takes me 2 minutes to verify. If what you have is already the strongest plan available in your area, I'll tell you and we're done. Fair?"*

**Pillar:** Reframing — redirect from "changing" to "verifying"

---

## "The devil you know feels safer."
*Variations: "Better safe than sorry." / "I'm not taking any chances." / "I'd rather stick with what I know."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — fear of the unknown, emotional resistance

**What's underneath:** Past experience — theirs or someone they know — where a change went wrong. The emotion is real and must be named before anything else can land.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Explaining Medicare mechanics — logic against fear almost always fails
- ❌ *"There's nothing to worry about."* — dismisses the concern without addressing it

**Correct first response:**
> *"I hear you — you've had a plan that's worked and you don't want to risk losing what you have. That makes complete sense. What I'm showing you doesn't take anything away. It just makes sure you're not leaving benefits on the table every month that could improve your quality of life."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — name the fear, redirect toward what they're currently leaving behind

---

## "I'm afraid if I switch I won't be able to get back."
*Variations: "What if I can't switch back?" / "What if I don't like the new plan — am I stuck?"*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — fear of no return

**What's underneath:** This client needs a safety net, not a Medicare education. The personal commitment is the answer — not enrollment periods.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Leading with a clinical explanation of enrollment windows — the objection is emotional and needs an emotional response first
- ❌ Any response that starts with compliance language before addressing the fear

**Correct first response:**
> *"I hear you — you don't want to make a move and find out you can't get back. That makes complete sense. Here's what I want you to know: if you enroll today and anything isn't right, you call me directly. I will work with you through the proper enrollment channels to resolve it — that's what I'm here for. I'm not asking you to leave forever. I'm asking you to see whether this puts $[amount] more in your pocket every month. That's it."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — name the fear, redirect with the personal accountability safety net

---

## "I just changed plans recently. I don't want to change again."
*Variations: "I already switched this year." / "I literally just enrolled in a new plan." / "I went through all that already."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — recency bias, switching fatigue

**What's underneath:** The client feels like they already did their homework. Changing again means they made a mistake — and nobody wants to feel that.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Pressing forward with a benefits comparison before acknowledging the fatigue
- ❌ Responding with plan names and network lists — this is an emotional objection, not an information gap

**Correct first response:**
> *"I completely understand — you just went through this and the last thing you want is more confusion. What I want to make sure is that the plan you moved to is actually giving you everything you're entitled to. Sometimes people switch to a plan that looks good on paper but isn't the best match for their specific situation. It takes 2 minutes for me to verify. If you're on the right plan, I'll tell you and you'll know for certain."*

**Note:** If the client enrolled during OEP (January 1 – March 31), they are eligible for one additional change during that window. Add: *"And because we're still in the Open Enrollment Period, you actually have a one-time window to review what you just enrolled in before it closes March 31st."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — validate the fatigue, reframe the purpose as verification not selling

---

## "I'm not making any decisions today."
*Variations: "I'm just looking." / "I'm not ready to do anything yet." / "I need more time."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — polite exit disguised as a pause

**What's underneath:** This is rarely about time. It's about not yet feeling safe enough to decide.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"Of course, take your time."* — validates the exit; the call is over
- ❌ Offering a callback as the first response

**Correct first response:**
> *"I hear you — and I'm not asking you to decide anything today. I'm asking you to look at a number. The decision you make after that is yours. Can I have 2 minutes?"*

**Pillar:** The Shift — reduce the perceived commitment to "look at a number," not "make a decision"

---

## "I want to keep my current plan — I know how it works."
*Variations: "I know what I have." / "I'm familiar with how it works." / "I don't want to learn a new system."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — comfort with familiarity

**What's underneath:** The effort of learning something new feels bigger than the benefit. More information increases perceived effort — it doesn't reduce resistance.

**Correct first response:**
> *"I completely respect that — and I want to make sure before I let you go: are you certain the plan you're on right now is still the same plan with the same benefits you originally chose? Plans change their terms every year, and sometimes what you're automatically continued on isn't the same as what you picked. It takes me 60 seconds to verify. If everything checks out, I'll tell you and we're done."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — reframe "staying on the same plan" as requiring confirmation

---

## "I'm happy with what I have. / I don't have any complaints."
*Variations: "Everything is fine." / "My plan is working for me." / "I have no problems with it."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — reflexive brush-off, satisfaction assumption

**What's underneath:** "Happy" is based on no comparison. The absence of complaints is not the same as receiving full value.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"But OK"* or *"That's great"* followed by backing off — this ends the call
- ❌ Arguing that their plan isn't actually good

**Correct first response:**
> *"I love hearing that — and I want to make sure that feeling is based on the full picture. What I'm looking at right now is that your current plan doesn't include [specific benefit]. That's $[amount] a year that's already funded for you — you're just not getting it. It takes 2 minutes to verify whether there's a plan in your area that gives you all of that without changing what you love about what you have. If I'm wrong, I'll tell you."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — reframe "happy" as "leaving money behind"

# SECTION 2: COMMERCIAL & BENEFIT OBJECTIONS — "I WANT THE MONEY I SAW ON TV"

> **Two very different callers live in this section. Read the signal before you respond.**
> 🔴 **Frustrated caller** — feels deceived by the commercial, angry or confused. Start with empathy. Name the frustration first. Do not pitch until they feel heard.
> 🟢 **Motivated caller** — excited, wants the benefit, ready to move. Skip the empathy. Go straight to verification and the Math Breakdown. Every second you spend explaining instead of enrolling is a second they can change their mind.

---

## "The commercial said A & B is all I need. Why do I need to enroll in anything?"
*Variations: "On TV they said I'd just get the money automatically." / "I heard I'd get $900 put back on my card." / "The commercial made it sound automatic."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — deceived by advertising, frustrated

**What's underneath:** This person called based on a TV commercial. They feel misled. The wrong response makes you part of the betrayal.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"I don't control the commercial."* — surrenders all authority; the client has no reason to trust anyone on this call
- ❌ *"That's not how it works — you can't just call and have a card mailed to you."* — shames the client for believing the commercial; triggers defensiveness

**Correct first response:**
> *"I completely understand why you feel that way — those commercials make it sound like the money comes straight from the government once you have A and B. What they don't explain is that the government gives those funds to specific high-tier plans to distribute. You've already done the hard part by getting Parts A and B. My job is to find which plan in your area pays out the highest amount and make sure that money lands in your hands instead of sitting unclaimed. Can I take 2 minutes to see what you qualify for?"*

**Pillar:** Reframing — join their frustration, become the ally against the commercial, position enrollment as the activation step

---

## "Why is what I qualify for so much less than what the commercial showed?"
*Variations: "They said $6,000 on TV — why am I only getting $300?" / "The commercial number was way higher."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — expectation mismatch

**Correct first response:**
> *"That's the most common frustration I hear — and you're right to be frustrated. Those commercials show the absolute maximum available anywhere in the country. Your actual benefit is based on your zip code and the plans available in your specific area. What I'm looking at right now for your area is $[amount] — and that's real. That's what hits your card every month starting [date]. The commercial number was the ceiling. This is your floor — and it's real."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — validate the frustration, anchor on the real number available

---

## "I saw a plan with $3,000 for dental. Yours only has $1,000."
*Variations: "Another plan I saw had higher benefits." / "I found something with more coverage."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — benefit comparison confusion

**What's underneath:** The client is comparing headline numbers without understanding the structure underneath them.

**Correct first response:**
> *"That's a great question — and the number matters, but so does the structure. A $3,000 allowance sounds better than $1,000 until you find out it's structured as a reimbursement only after a deductible, or the network is so limited your dentist isn't in it. What I want to make sure of is that the plan I'm showing you actually gets you more money in your pocket — not just a bigger number on paper. Let me show you the comparison."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — total value, not headline number

---

## "I heard about a card that gives me $200 a month for groceries."
*Variations: "I want the grocery card." / "Someone told me I could get money for food." / "I'm calling about the food card I saw advertised."*

**Signal:** 🟢 GREEN — motivated caller with a specific benefit in mind. This is the lead.

**Correct first response:**
> *"That benefit is real — and the exact amount depends on the specific plan available in your zip code and whether you qualify. Let me check your eligibility right now. If you qualify for a Special Needs Plan, the grocery benefit can be significant. If not, I'll show you the strongest alternative available in your area. Either way, you'll have the real number in about 60 seconds."*

Then immediately verify and execute The Math Breakdown.

**Pillar:** Persuasion — confirm, verify, pivot

---

## "I want the Flex Card they showed on TV that I can use anywhere."
*Variations: "I want the card that pays for everything." / "The commercial said I could use it for bills."*

**Signal:** 🟢 GREEN with an expectation gap

**Correct first response:**
> *"That card is real — it's typically a pre-loaded benefit card for things like groceries, OTC health items, utilities, and dental depending on the plan. The key is making sure the plan I put you on has the highest card value available for your specific situation and that everything you actually spend money on is covered. Let me pull up what's available in your area right now."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — manage the expectation, redirect toward the real value

---

## "My doctor told me I can't switch plans."
*Variations: "The doctor's office said I have to stay on this plan." / "My physician told me this plan is required."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — false authority from a trusted source

**What's underneath:** Doctor's office staff almost never understand Medicare plan contracting. The "mandate" is almost certainly misinformation.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Arguing directly with what the doctor's office said

**Correct first response:**
> *"I hear you — and I want to make sure you have the right information before you decide anything. Let me check Dr. [name]'s status live right now, while we're on the phone. Doctors' offices sometimes aren't up to date on which plans they're contracted with — those contracts change every year. Give me 60 seconds."*

**If in-network:** *"Dr. [name] is covered. You keep your doctor and you get [benefit]."*
**If out-of-network:** *"Dr. [name] isn't on that plan — let me check what other plans cover them."*

**Pillar:** Persuasion — live verification is the authority; show, don't argue

---

## "I was told I'd lose my Medicaid if I switch."
*Variations: "Someone said switching plans would affect my Medicaid." / "I don't want to mess up my Medicaid."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — false fear, high stakes

**Correct first response:**
> *"I completely understand why that would stop you — and I want to make sure you have the right information. Medicaid and your Medicare Advantage plan are two separate programs. Switching your Medicare plan does not affect your Medicaid status. Your Medicaid is based on your income — not which Medicare plan you choose. I can verify your eligibility right now while we're on the phone. You won't lose a single dollar of Medicaid by looking at what's available."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — name the fear, dismantle the false belief with fact, verify live

---

## "I was denied before / I tried this before and it didn't work."
*Variations: "I applied for a plan like this and got turned down." / "Someone told me I don't qualify."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — past failure experience

**Correct first response:**
> *"I hear you — and I want to find out what happened. Eligibility changes every year, and it depends on the specific plan. Just because one plan or one situation said no doesn't mean every option in your area says no. I can check eligibility right now for everything available in your zip code. If there's something you qualify for that you were told you didn't, I want you to have it. Can I check?"*

**Pillar:** Persuasion — make checking feel low-risk; position yourself as the verification authority

# SECTION 3: STALL OBJECTIONS

---

## "I need to think about it." / "Call me back later."
*Variations: "Let me sleep on it." / "I need to process this." / "I just need some time."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — polite exit disguised as a pause

**What's underneath:** This is almost never about needing time. It's about inaction not yet feeling expensive enough. The client doesn't have a compelling reason to decide now.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"Of course — I'll call you back."* — the lead is almost never recovered once the call ends this way
- ❌ *"What specifically do you need to think about?"* — open-ended; gives them room to manufacture more objections

**Correct first response:**
> *"I completely respect that — and I want to make sure you're thinking about the right thing. The question isn't 'should I switch.' It's: do you want $[annual amount] back in your pocket this year, or do you want to keep giving that to your insurance company? If there's something specific giving you pause, let's work through it right now together — because I'd hate for you to leave that benefit unclaimed just to keep thinking about it."*

If they push back again:
> *"What's the one thing that would need to be true for you to feel confident moving forward today?"*

**Pillar:** The Shift — reframe what they're "thinking about"; make inaction the cost

---

## "Please send me something in the mail."
*Variations: "Mail me some information." / "Send me a brochure." / "I want to see it in writing first."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — exit disguised as a request

**What's underneath:** They want a dignified way to end the call. The request for mail is not genuine information-seeking — it's an escape hatch.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Offering to mail anything — this legitimizes the exit and eliminates urgency
- ❌ *"I can't do that"* without explanation — sounds like an obstacle

**Correct first response:**
> *"I hear you — you want to see it in writing before you do anything. Here's the thing: the official plan documents only get generated once an application is on file — that's how the system works. What I can do is submit the application today, which triggers those documents to arrive at your door within a few days. You'll have everything in writing. And if anything in those documents doesn't match what I've told you today, you call me directly and we sort it out. The application is how you get the paperwork — and it comes with your coverage starting [date] and $[monthly benefit] on the way."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — enrollment generates the documentation; reframe "send me something" as enrollment itself

---

## "I want to look it up online first. / Give me the plan number."
*Variations: "Give me the plan ID so I can research it." / "I'll check it out on the website."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — exiting with information as the excuse

**What's underneath:** The client is constructing an exit. Providing the plan ID hands them everything they need to walk away — and nothing they need to stay.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Providing the plan ID or website URL — this tells the client you are not necessary for this transaction

**Correct first response:**
> *"I hear you — you want to verify this yourself, and I respect that. Here's the thing: you're on the phone right now with someone who has direct system access to every plan available in your zip code. I can pull what would take you an hour to find on your own and have it in front of you in 60 seconds. The research is done. All I'm asking is 2 minutes for you to look at the numbers and decide for yourself. If you don't like what you see, we're done. Fair?"*

**Pillar:** Persuasion — position yourself as the tool that completes their research faster than they can do it alone

---

## "I want to talk to my sister / son / daughter / friend first."
*Variations: "Let me ask someone I trust." / "I don't make these decisions alone." / "Let me run it by my family."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — deflection / false safety net

**What's underneath:** When someone names a person but can't name a specific question, the consultation is a deflection. If they can name a real question, answer it right now.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"Of course — I'll call you back after you talk to them."* — once the call ends, you rarely get back in

**Correct first response — test the objection first:**
> *"Absolutely — what questions would you be asking them so I can make sure I'm better prepared for when we speak again?"*

The client will either name a question — which you answer right there — or they can't, and the deflection dissolves. If the third party is genuinely available now:
> *"Is [person] available right now? I'd love to walk both of you through the numbers together. These decisions are always better when everyone hears the same information at the same time."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — test the deflection before offering a callback; bring them in now if genuine

---

## "I'm not sure if I'll qualify."
*Variations: "I might not even be eligible." / "I probably don't qualify for the extras."*

**Signal:** 🟡 YELLOW — uncertainty stall

**What's underneath:** Fear of going through the process and being told no.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"You probably qualify."* — reassurance without verification has no authority

**Correct first response:**
> *"That's actually why I'm here — I can verify eligibility right now while we're on the phone. You don't have to wonder. If you qualify, we'll know in 60 seconds. If you don't, I'll tell you exactly why and what your options are. Either way you get a clear answer. Is that fair?"*

**Pillar:** Persuasion — assume the close, position yourself as the verification authority

# SECTION 4: THIRD-PARTY OBJECTIONS

---

## "My spouse isn't home. I need to talk to them first."
*Variations: "I need to check with my husband/wife." / "My spouse handles these things." / "My wife/husband would want to know about this."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — spousal deference

**What's underneath:** The client wants to protect the relationship and doesn't want to make a unilateral financial decision. This can be real or a polite exit — test it first.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ *"You can always loop them in later."*
- ❌ Scheduling a callback as the first response

**Correct first response — test it:**
> *"That makes complete sense — this is a family decision. What specifically would you want to run by them? Because if I can answer that right now, you'll both have the same information."*

If the consultation is genuine, bring them in immediately:
> *"Is [spouse] reachable right now? I can walk both of you through the numbers together in about 5 minutes — that way they hear it directly from me and you're not playing telephone. What's the best number to reach them?"*

**Do not offer a callback.** Either answer the question on the spot or bring the third party in now.

**Pillar:** Persuasion — bring them in; don't let the sale die outside the call

---

## "My daughter/son handles my insurance. They tell me not to touch it."
*Variations: "My kids set this up for me." / "My family manages these things." / "I'm not supposed to make changes without them."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — family loyalty / fear of conflict

**What's underneath:** The client doesn't want to make a unilateral decision and risk family conflict. The family member is a gatekeeper — not necessarily an obstacle if approached correctly.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Continuing to sell to the client alone when a family member holds the real decision
- ❌ *"You can always talk to them after."*

**Correct first response:**
> *"I love that you want to include your family — that's a great sign. Here's what I'd suggest: can we get [son/daughter] on the phone right now? I'd rather explain this to both of you at the same time so they hear the numbers directly from me. A lot of times, family members are the ones who say 'why didn't you do this sooner?' once they see the math. Is [person] available?"*

**Pillar:** Persuasion — turn the potential veto into an ally; bring them in now

---

## "I'll have my [family member] call you."
*Variations: "Have my son call you back." / "My daughter will reach out."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — total deferral; the call is ending

**What's underneath:** The family member will call when they get around to it — which is often never.

**Correct first response:**
> *"I understand — and I want to make this as easy as possible. Before we hang up, let me give you the one number that matters: [current benefit] vs. [new benefit] = $[difference] more per year. That's the conversation to have with them. And if [family member] has questions, I'm available to speak with them directly — here's my number."*

**Pillar:** Refocusing — arm them with the closing language; make the callback informed and inevitable

---

## "My insurance lady / agent already handles this for me."
*Variations: "I have an agent who takes care of this." / "I'm already working with someone."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — relationship loyalty

**What's underneath:** The client's loyalty is to a person, not a plan. Attacking the agent directly is the fastest way to lose the call.

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ Agreeing with the client and suggesting they talk to their current agent — this hands the sale to a competitor
- ❌ Criticizing the other agent directly

**Correct first response — if a specific gap exists (uncovered doctor, missing benefit):**
> *"I completely respect loyalty — and I'm not here to replace anyone. Here's what I want you to know: your current plan doesn't cover Dr. [name]. If you see them this week, you're looking at a bill you shouldn't have to pay. Your current agent either didn't know or didn't have access to a plan that fixes this. I'm not criticizing them — I just want you to have coverage that actually works. Can we fix this today?"*

**If no specific gap exists:**
> *"Your current agent cares about you — and so do I. The difference is I'm looking at what's available right now and I see $[amount] a month you're leaving on the table. A good agent would want you to have that. Let's take 2 minutes and look."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — validate the loyalty, identify the gap the other agent left, position yourself as the solution

---

## "I don't like to make these decisions over the phone."
*Variations: "I prefer to do things in person." / "I don't trust phone calls for this." / "I've been scammed before."*

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — trust barrier

**What's underneath:** Almost always about trust, not channel.

**What NOT to say:**

## The Escalation Sequence — When the Standard Responses Don't Work

*For when you've deployed the first response and they're still not moving. Use this in order.*

**Push 1 — The 1-to-10 Scale** (use first):
> *"On a scale of 1 to 10, how confident are you that your current plan is the best one available in your area right now? If you're a 10, we're done. If there's any doubt, let's use that 2 minutes."*

**Push 2 — The Waiting Tax** (use if Push 1 doesn't land):
> *"Every month you stay where you are, $[amount] that belongs to you goes unclaimed. That's not staying safe — that's choosing to leave it behind. I want to make sure that's a choice you're making consciously."*

**Push 3 — The Specific Dollar Question** (use if Push 2 doesn't land):
> *"Before I let you go — what would you do with $[annualized amount] if it were coming to you every year? Just tell me what it would mean."*

If they answer — that is your humanization material. Use it immediately for Step 3 of The Math Breakdown and return to the close.

**Graceful Exit** (use only after all three pushes have failed):
> *"I completely respect that — and I want you to know the door is always open. If anything changes with your plan or your situation, call me directly. My number is [number]. You deserve to have the right plan — and when you're ready, I'll be here."*

**Never end a call angry, dismissive, or abrupt.** The graceful exit preserves the relationship. Some clients call back. Most don't — but none should hang up feeling pressured or disrespected.

# SECTION 12: MOMENTUM KILLERS — AGENT-CREATED DEAD SPOTS

*These are not client objections. They are situations where the agent creates resistance through their own behavior.*

---

## Dead Air During System Navigation

**What's happening:** The agent goes silent while searching the system. Silence feels like abandonment — and allows internal resistance to build.

**What it costs:** Even a 30-second silence can shift a warm call cold.

**The fix — narrate while navigating:**
> *"While I pull this up — tell me, are you using all the benefits on your current plan? A lot of people don't realize some of them expire if you don't use them."*

> *"While I'm searching — real quick, are you paying out of pocket for any of your medications right now?"*

Never go silent. Keep the client engaged while you work.

---

## Permission-Seeking Language

**The phrases that kill the sale. Remove them permanently.**

- *"I'm not here to strong-arm you."* — plants the idea of pressure where there was none
- *"Please say yes."* — complete loss of authority
- *"Would it be okay if I..."* — hands control to the client at the worst moment
- *"Is there any reason you wouldn't want to move forward?"* — invites the client to manufacture objections
- *"How does that sound?"* — turns a statement into a request for approval

**The fix:** State and move.
> ❌ *"Would you like me to compare the two plans?"*
> ✅ *"Let me show you exactly what this looks like side by side."*

---

## Rapport Without a Transition

**What's happening:** The call has become a social conversation. The client likes you. But no enrollment is happening.

**What it costs:** A willing client who runs out of time or energy before the enrollment is a lost enrollment.

**The fix — The Bridge Back:**
> *"I love that — and that's actually exactly why this matters for you..."*
> *"Real quick — based on what you just told me, let me show you something..."*
> *"That connects perfectly to what I need to show you about your plan..."*

When a client mentions paying out of pocket for something, expresses a health frustration, or mentions financial stress — that is a Close Trigger. Stop the rapport and move to the application.

---

## Missing the Close Confirmation

**What's happening:** The enrollment is submitted but the client hangs up uncertain.

**What it costs:** An uncertain enrollment is a disenrollment waiting to happen. Twenty seconds changes that outcome.

**The fix — Close Confirmation after every single enrollment:**
> *"Before I let you go — I want you to know that starting [date], $[amount] comes back to you every single month. That's yours. You made the right call today. If you have any questions between now and then, call me directly."*

The close is not the end of the sale. The close confirmation is.

---

## When the Offer Is Weak — Handling a Low-Benefit Area

**What's happening:** The plans available in the client's zip code are not significantly better than what they already have. The math doesn't make a compelling case. This happens.

**What it costs if handled wrong:** Pushing a weak offer destroys trust and creates disenrollments. Overpromising and underdelivering is worse than not enrolling at all.

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Inflate the benefit or downplay the difference to force an enrollment
- ❌ Pretend the numbers are stronger than they are

**The honest pivot:**
> *"I want to be straight with you — the difference in your area isn't as large as it is in some zip codes. What I'm looking at is $[real number], which is $[annualized]. It's not dramatic. What I can tell you is that it's real, it's funded, and it's yours to claim. Whether that's worth 5 minutes to you is completely your call — and if you tell me it's not, I'll respect that."*

**If the benefit is genuinely not worth switching:**
> *"Based on what I'm seeing in your area, I don't think a switch makes sense for you right now. Your current plan is actually competitive here. What I'd recommend is calling me back during Annual Enrollment — plans restructure every year and the picture can change. I'll note your situation and reach back out."*

**The principle:** Integrity on a weak call builds more trust than a forced enrollment. Clients who feel you were honest with them when you didn't have to be will call back. Clients who feel pushed into a bad deal will disenroll, dispute, and tell their family.

# SECTION 13: UNIVERSAL RESPONSES — HAVE THESE READY

*These work across all call types. Memorize them.*

---

**When you need one sentence to hold the call:**
> *"I hear you — and I want to make sure I understand. What's the one thing that would need to be true for you to feel confident moving forward today?"*

**When you need to reset without losing momentum:**
> *"Let me step back for a second and make sure I'm showing you the right thing."*

**When the client gives you a close trigger:**
> *"You mentioned [what they said]. That's exactly what this plan was built for. Here's what that means for you in real numbers..."*

**When a client asks about switching back or what happens if something's wrong:**
> *"If you enroll today and something isn't right, call me directly. I will work to find a resolution through the proper enrollment channels. That's what I'm here for."*

**When the client says "That sounds too good to be true":**
> *"I completely understand that reaction — and the reason it sounds that way is because most people in your situation have been leaving benefits unclaimed for years without knowing it. What I'm showing you isn't new money. It's your money that's been sitting there."*

**When the client goes quiet:**
> *"I want to make sure I explained that clearly — what questions do you have about what I just showed you?"*

**When the benefit in their area is less than what the commercial showed:**
> *"Those commercials show the national maximum. Your actual benefit is based on your zip code and your specific plan options. What I found for your area is $[amount] — and that's real money that starts [date]."*

**When facing an absolute hard no at the end of a call:**
> *"I completely respect that — and before we hang up, I want to make sure you know: if your situation changes or your plan changes its benefits next year, you can always call back. My number is [number]. You deserve to have the right plan."*

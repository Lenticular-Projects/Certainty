# THE CERTAINTY SYSTEM
## 9 Call Type Playbook
### Agent Quick Reference | Live Call Use

---

> **How to use this:** In the first two minutes of every call, identify the primary type. That tells you the playbook. Most calls are combinations — identify the dominant one first and lead with that. Keep this open. When a client pushes back, look up what you just heard.

## THE 9 CALL TYPES — QUICK REFERENCE

| # | Type | First Signal | Primary Pillar | Biggest Mistake |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Money Caller | 🟢 GREEN | The Shift | Stopping at the monthly number |
| 2 | Scared Switcher | 🔴 RED | Reframing | Logic response to emotional fear |
| 3 | Misinformed | 🔴 RED | Reframing + Persuasion | Building on a false foundation |
| 4 | Third Party Controlled | 🟢 + gatekeeper | Persuasion | Selling to the wrong person |
| 5 | Detail Staller | 🟢/🔴 mix | The Shift | Giving them something to research |
| 6 | Time Bomb | 🟢 + countdown | Refocusing | Spending time on rapport |
| 7 | Commercial Myth Caller | 🔴 RED (betrayal) | Reframing | Defending the system |
| 8 | Veteran | 🔴 RED (fear of loss) | The Shift | PCP before neutralizing VA fear |
| 9 | Timing Objector | 🔴 RED (stall) | The Shift | Accepting the stall and offering a callback |

**The Four Pillars — quick definitions:**
- **Persuasion:** Lead with authority. Assumptive frame. Never ask permission to advance.
- **Reframing:** Name the emotion first. Redirect the meaning. Agree with the feeling, change what it points to.
- **The Shift:** Change what they're comparing against. Show them what they're losing, not what they'd gain. Always includes the Math Breakdown.
- **Refocusing:** Validate in one sentence. Bridge back with a forward-moving statement. Narrate system navigation to kill dead air.

## TYPE 1 — THE MONEY CALLER

**The two types you will encounter most are Type 1 and Type 2. Know these cold.**

### Who This Is
They called because of a specific benefit — the grocery card, the OTC allowance, the Part B giveback, or something they saw on TV. They are the most motivated caller you will get. They are not resistant. They are ready — as long as you do the math completely and connect it to their life.

The danger with this call type is not resistance. It is the agent stopping at Step 2 of the Math Breakdown and assuming the number speaks for itself. It does not. Step 3 is the close.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"I'm calling about the card I saw on TV."*
- *"Someone told me I could get money for groceries."*
- *"I heard I could get money put back on my Social Security."*
- *"I want to see if I qualify for the food card."*
- *"I'm calling about the $1,200 a month I saw advertised."*
- *"Does Medicare pay you money? I heard it can."*

### Watch For: Type 1 Presenting as Type 7
A Money Caller who has already heard the $6,000 or $900 number from a commercial — and is expecting it — is no longer a pure Type 1. They are now a Type 7 before you've said a word. If you hear any version of *"the commercial said..."* in the first 30 seconds, defuse the expectation gap first before executing The Math Breakdown. Skipping the defuse and going straight to math makes you another person who's part of the problem. See Type 7 for the first move.

### Primary Move
Full 3-step Math Breakdown. Do not stop at Step 2. Step 3 — connecting the annual number to something real this specific person told you about their life — is the close.

**Step 1:** State the comparison. Current benefit vs. available benefit. Side by side, clear numbers.
**Step 2:** Annualize it. $35/month sounds minor. $420/year lands. Always multiply by 12.
**Step 3:** Humanize it. Connect the annual number to something they told you. *"You mentioned your grocery bill. That's $420 a year going back into that."* This is the close.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "I want the $3,000 card I saw on TV. Yours only has $200."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — expectation mismatch

**What's underneath:** The commercial set an expectation. The reality feels like a downgrade. They're not being difficult — they're disappointed.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"I don't control the commercial."* — surrenders all authority; the call is now over
- *"That number isn't real."* — shames them for believing it; triggers defensiveness
- *"Well, $200 is still good."* — validates their disappointment and agrees the offer is less

**✅ Option A — when the real benefit is solid:**
> *"You're right to call that out — those commercials show the national maximum, wherever in the country the benefit is highest. Your actual benefit is based on your specific zip code and what plans are available there. What I'm looking at for your area right now is $[amount] — and that's real. That hits your card every month starting [date]. The commercial showed the ceiling. I'm showing you your floor — and it's real money you can use."*

**✅ Option B — pivot to total value:**
> *"That makes sense — and here's what those commercials don't show you. The total value isn't just one benefit. When I add up the OTC allowance, the Part B giveback, and what you're currently paying in deductibles, the full picture looks like this: [run the full Pivot]. The grocery card is part of it. The whole thing together is what matters."*

**✅ Option C — when the card amount in their area is genuinely limited:**
> *"I hear you — and I'm not going to pretend the card is $3,000 in your area, because it's not. What I can show you is everything else on the table: [deductible savings, MOOP reduction, additional benefits]. When you add it all up, the real number is [annualized total]. That's what I want you to leave this call knowing."*

**Pillar:** Reframing → The Shift

---

### OBJECTION 2 — "I want to wait and see if I can get Medicaid first."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — false safety net

**What's underneath:** The client has decided Medicaid is the real prize and everything else is second-best. In most cases, they have already been denied or face income thresholds that will not change.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- Accepting the wait as a legitimate reason to end the call
- *"OK, call me back when you find out."* — this sale is gone

**✅ Option A:**
> *"I hear you — and here's what I want you to think about while that process is going on: if your income hasn't changed, the Medicaid result is likely the same. While you're waiting, you're leaving $[monthly] every single month on the table — that's $[annualized] a year that you qualify for right now and aren't getting. Let's secure what you're eligible for today. If Medicaid comes through later, we can always adjust. But today, that money is already funded and sitting there."*

**✅ Option B:**
> *"Medicaid is worth pursuing — and it doesn't have to be either/or. This plan is available to you right now regardless of how the Medicaid application goes. The benefit you called about — $[amount] — starts [date]. Every month we wait, that's money that doesn't come back. What if we secure this today and you keep the Medicaid process going on the side?"*

**Pillar:** The Shift — make waiting expensive in specific dollars

---

### OBJECTION 3 — "I heard the card pays for rent and utilities. Does this one do that?"

**Signal:** 🟢 GREEN with expectation gap

**What's underneath:** They want to know if the card is flexible. This is not resistance — this is a motivated buyer asking a qualifying question.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"Great question — and the answer depends on the specific plan. Some flex cards in your area cover groceries, OTC health items, utilities, and in some cases rent or transportation. Let me check exactly what's included for your zip code right now — that way you know exactly what you're getting before we do anything. Give me just a second."*

Then verify and connect what you find to their stated needs before moving forward.

**Pillar:** Persuasion — confirm, verify, advance

## TYPE 2 — THE SCARED SWITCHER
*(includes Brand Loyalist and Relationship Loyalist as sub-types)*

**The two types you will encounter most are Type 1 and Type 2. Know these cold.**

### Who This Is
Interested but afraid of losing something. Their doctor. Their plan. Their carrier. Their "insurance lady." Fear is the objection — not the words they say. The words are a cover. Fear of change, fear of loss, fear of making a mistake that hurts their health or their routine.

The three variations under this type share the same first move: **name the fear before you present anything.** The sub-type tells you what they're afraid of losing — a carrier, a person, or just stability in general.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"I'm happy with what I have."*
- *"I've had this plan for years and it works for me."*
- *"I don't want to change."*
- *"The devil you know is better than the devil you don't."*
- *"I love [carrier] — they've taken care of me."*
- *"I need to talk to my insurance lady first."*
- *"My daughter set this up for me and I don't want to mess with it."*

### Primary Move
Name the fear before presenting anything. Do not lead with logic, math, or product information. The emotion has to be addressed first, or nothing else lands.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "I don't want to change. My plan works."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — fear of change

**What's underneath:** They have connected their current plan to their health and their safety. "My plan works" means "I'm afraid something will stop working if I change."

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- Explaining why the new plan is better — logic against fear does not work
- *"But your plan is costing you money."* — sounds like an attack; they defend it
- *"You're missing out on a lot of benefits."* — guilt framing without credibility

**✅ Option A:**
> *"I hear you — and I'm not here to change anything unless it's clearly better. What I want to do is take 2 minutes and make sure you're getting everything that's available to you right now. If what you have is already the best option in your area, I'll tell you that and we're done. But if there's something sitting there you're not getting — that's what I want you to know."*

**✅ Option B:**
> *"I completely understand — you've had something that works and you don't want to risk it. What I'm showing you doesn't take that away. It adds to it. The doctors you have, the coverage you're used to — that stays. What changes is that you also get [specific benefit]. Can I show you that in 60 seconds?"*

**✅ Option C — when they seem final:**
> *"Before I let you go — on a scale of 1 to 10, how confident are you that your current plan is the strongest one available in your area this year? Plans change every year. If you're a 10, we're done and I respect that. If there's any doubt, it takes 2 minutes to verify."*

**Pillar:** Reframing

---

### OBJECTION 2 — Brand Loyalist sub-type: "I love [Humana / UHC / Aetna]. I'm not switching."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — carrier attachment

**What's underneath:** They've connected the brand to their health. It's emotional, not rational. Attacking the brand means attacking their past decisions.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- Presenting a competitor plan as the first move
- Calling their current plan "inferior" — this ends the call; when you insult a plan, they defend it
- *"If you're not willing to change, there's nothing I can do for you."* — hands them the exit

**✅ Option A — same-brand upgrade exists (always try this first):**
> *"I love that [carrier] has taken such good care of you — that matters. My job today isn't to move you away from [carrier]. It's to make sure you're on the right [carrier] plan for this year. They have multiple plans in your area and some pay significantly more than others. Can I take 2 minutes to show you what else [carrier] has available?"*

**✅ Option B — introducing a different carrier:**
> *"I completely respect that. Before you go — I'm looking at your current plan and I'm seeing $[amount] a year you're not getting. If I can show you a plan where your exact doctors are covered and the number is better, would you at least want to see the comparison? You make the call. I just want you to have the full picture."*

**Pillar:** Reframing → The Shift

---

### OBJECTION 3 — Relationship Loyalist sub-type: "I have an insurance lady / agent who handles this."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — person loyalty

**What's underneath:** Their loyalty is to a person, not a plan. The other agent has a relationship advantage. Do not attack that person — you will lose.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"You should go talk to them."* — handing the sale directly to a competitor
- Criticizing the other agent openly — they will defend the person they trust

**✅ Option A — when a specific gap exists:**
> *"I completely respect that loyalty — and I'm not here to replace anyone. Here's what I want you to know: your current plan doesn't cover [specific thing]. Your current agent either didn't know or didn't have access to a plan that fixes this. I'm not criticizing them — I just want you to have coverage that actually works. Can we fix this today?"*

**✅ Option B — no specific gap yet identified:**
> *"Your current agent clearly cares about you — and so do I. The difference is I'm looking at what's available right now, and I see $[amount] a month you're leaving on the table. A good agent would want you to have that. Let's take 2 minutes and look at the numbers."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — validate the loyalty, identify the gap, position yourself as the solution

## TYPE 3 — THE MISINFORMED

### Who This Is
Operating on false information from someone they trusted — a doctor's office receptionist, a family member, a previous agent, a TV commercial. The false belief is the wall. No selling works until it comes down. And it has to come down without making them feel stupid for believing it.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"My doctor said I can't change my plan."*
- *"I heard if I switch I lose my doctor."*
- *"My daughter told me this plan is the best one."*
- *"A and B is all I need — they said so on TV."*
- *"The other agent told me I can't do better than what I have."*

### Primary Move
Live verification using your system access as the authority. *"Let me check that right now while we're on the phone."* You are not arguing with them — you are showing them. The screen is the authority, not you vs. them.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "My doctor said I have to stay on my current plan."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — authority-based false belief

**What's underneath:** A trusted authority (their doctor, or more often a receptionist) told them something incorrect. Their loyalty to that source is higher than their skepticism of the claim.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"No, ma'am, that's not true."* — confrontational; triggers defensiveness; they dig in
- *"Your doctor is wrong."* — attacking a trusted relationship; call ends
- Accepting it and trying to work around it — you are building on sand

**✅ Option A:**
> *"I hear you — and your doctor's office knows your health better than anyone. What they may not have meant is that you're locked out of better coverage. Doctors' offices sometimes say that to mean 'we work with your current plan' — not that other plans don't cover you. Let me check your doctor's network status right now, live, so we're both looking at the same thing. This takes 60 seconds."*

**✅ Option B — when verification confirms the doctor is covered:**
> *"I've got Dr. [name] right here — they're in-network with this plan. Same doctor, same care. What changes is that you also get [benefit]. The concern your doctor's office had? It's resolved. Do you want to take a look at the plan?"*

**Pillar:** Reframing → Persuasion — verify live; your system is the authority

---

### OBJECTION 2 — "I was told if I switch I lose my doctor."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — network fear

**What's underneath:** Fear of disrupting medical care. This is real. Respect it — then resolve it live.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"That's exactly what I want to verify before we do anything. Tell me who you see regularly and I'll check them right now while we're on the phone. If your doctor isn't covered, I'll tell you and we stop there. If they are — and I'm expecting they will be — then that concern is resolved and we can look at what's available."*

Run the check. Show them the result. Do not advance until the network fear is resolved.

**Pillar:** Persuasion — verify live, don't argue

---

### OBJECTION 3 — "The commercial said A and B is all I need."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — deceived by advertising

**What's underneath:** They called based on a TV ad. They feel misled now that the reality is more complex. The wrong response makes you part of the deception.

> ⚠️ **If this is the primary reason they called — not just a background belief but the thing that got them on the phone — you are likely in a Type 7 call.** Go there first. Type 3 handles clients who believe this as background information. Type 7 handles clients for whom the commercial disappointment is the emotional state of the entire call.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"I don't control the commercial."* — surrenders all authority; now no one on this call is trustworthy
- *"That's not how it works — you can't just have a card mailed to you."* — shames them for believing it

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"I completely understand why you feel that way — those commercials make it sound like the money comes straight to you once you have Parts A and B. What they leave out is that the government gives those funds to specific plans to distribute. You've already done the hard part by getting Parts A and B. My job is to find which plan in your area pays out the highest amount and make sure that money actually gets to you instead of sitting unclaimed. Can I take 2 minutes to check what you qualify for?"*

**Pillar:** Reframing — join the frustration; become the ally against the commercial; position enrollment as the activation step

## TYPE 4 — THE THIRD PARTY CONTROLLED

### Who This Is
The person on the phone is not the one making the decision. A spouse, adult child, sibling, caregiver, or POA holds the real authority. The client will say yes — and then check with that person after you hang up. That conversation, without you in it, is where the sale dies.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"I need to talk to my daughter first."*
- *"My husband handles all of this."*
- *"Let me ask my son — he set up my last plan."*
- You can hear another voice in the background giving instructions or commentary.
- The client keeps pausing to relay what you're saying to someone else.

### Primary Move
Identify the real decision maker early. Get them on the call — or sell to both of them simultaneously right now. The goal is: before you hang up, the third party has heard the case directly from you.

> *"It sounds like [family member] is important to this decision — do you think they could jump on real quick? I'd love to walk both of you through this at the same time so no one has to play telephone afterward."*

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "I need to talk to my daughter / son / husband first."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — decision authority elsewhere

**What's underneath:** They're not saying no. They're saying they don't have permission to say yes alone.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"OK, I'll call you back."* — this call is now over; you will not get them back
- Continuing to sell as if the third party doesn't exist

**✅ Option A — try to get them on now:**
> *"Absolutely — is there any chance they're available right now? It would take 5 minutes and then you'd both have the same information. That way no one has to relay anything and you can make the decision together."*

**✅ Option B — can't get them on now:**
> *"That makes total sense — and I want to make this easy for that conversation. Here are the three things that matter: [benefit 1], [benefit 2], [benefit 3]. The question your daughter is going to ask is whether your doctors are covered — and they are. Dr. [name] is confirmed in-network. I'll give you my direct number. When you talk to her, if she has questions, have her call me."*

**✅ Option C — arm them for the conversation:**
> *"Before you go — what questions do you think [family member] is going to ask? Let's answer them right now so you walk into that conversation ready."*
*(If the client can't name a question, the objection often dissolves. If they can, you answer it before they leave.)*

**Pillar:** Persuasion — hold the lead; arm them for the third party conversation

---

### OBJECTION 2 — Third party is on the call and is skeptical or cautious.

**Signal:** 🔴 RED from the third party, not the primary caller

**What's underneath:** The third party came to protect their family member. Treat them as a partner, not an obstacle. They are often more rational and less emotional than the primary caller — facts land here.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"[Name], I'm really glad you're on the call — it's the right move to be involved in this. My job is to make sure [primary caller] has the strongest plan available in their area. Here's what I've found: [present the case]. The math works — and your [parent/spouse] stays with the same doctors. What questions do you have?"*

**Pillar:** Persuasion — shift your audience, maintain the lead

---

### OBJECTION 3 — Third party is actively blocking the call.

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — hostile gatekeeper, not just cautious

**What's underneath:** The family member isn't evaluating — they're ending the call on behalf of their loved one. This is the hardest version of this call. Pressure will make it worse. Respect and a direct question are the only moves.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- Continuing to pitch — they are not listening and you are now in conflict
- Backing down and offering a callback — the sale is gone and won't come back

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"I completely respect that — and I can hear you're looking out for [name]. That's exactly the right instinct. I just want to ask you one question directly: is there a specific concern about this plan, or about me, that I can address right now? Because if there's something I haven't explained clearly, I'd rather fix that than have [name] miss out on $[amount] a year over a misunderstanding."*

If they soften, address the concern directly and re-engage both callers together. If they hold firm, give your number to the primary caller directly: *"[Primary caller's name] — my number is [number]. If you want to revisit this on your own time, I'm here."* Then close the call cleanly.

**Pillar:** Persuasion → Reframing — respect the gatekeeper; isolate the real objection; leave the door open with the primary caller

## TYPE 5 — THE DETAIL STALLER

### Who This Is
Intellectually engaged but emotionally exiting. "I want to think about it," "send me something," and "I want to look it up" sound like requests for information. They are not. They are polite goodbyes. An agent who treats the stall as a real information request validates the exit and hands the client a way out.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"Can you send me something in writing?"*
- *"I want to look this up online first."*
- *"I want to think about it and call you back."*
- *"What's the plan ID? I'll research it."*
- *"I'm not ready to make a decision today."*

### Primary Move
Make inaction expensive in specific dollars. Then make enrollment the safe, low-risk move — not a commitment. The enrollment is the documentation. Do not give them a plan ID, a website, or a reason to leave.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "I want to think about it."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — polite exit

**What's underneath:** Fear of making a mistake. Not a request for time.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"No problem, take your time."* — validates the exit; the call is over
- Providing a plan ID or website to research — now they have what they need and they don't need you

**✅ Option A:**
> *"I completely understand — and I want to make this easy. Here's what I want you to think about: every month you stay on your current plan, you're leaving $[amount] on the table. That's $[annualized] a year. The question isn't really whether this plan is good — the math is right there. The question is whether you want to stop leaving that money behind starting [date] or wait another month. What specifically would you want to think through?"*

**✅ Option B — neutralize the risk:**
> *"Here's the thing about thinking about it — the enrollment protects you. If you enroll today and something isn't right, call me directly. I will work to find a resolution through the proper enrollment channels. That's what I'm here for. The risk of waiting is $[amount] a month you don't get back. The risk of enrolling is zero, because I'm here."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — make inaction expensive; remove the risk of enrolling

---

### OBJECTION 2 — "Send me something in writing first."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — exit disguised as a request

**What's underneath:** They want to feel in control. They want something tangible. But they rarely read what gets sent, and if they do, there's no agent on the other end to handle objections.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"Sure, what's your email?"* — the sale just ended
- Giving them the plan ID to look up themselves — you've removed yourself from the equation

**✅ Option A:**
> *"Absolutely — and the most important document is the plan itself, which comes 7 to 14 business days after enrollment. It covers everything: benefits, network, costs. The enrollment IS the documentation. Once it's in hand, you can review every detail. And if anything doesn't match what I told you today, you call me directly and we fix it."*

**✅ Option B:**
> *"I can make sure you get everything in writing — and the best way to do that is through the enrollment, because that triggers the plan packet with all the details. Walking you through a brochure right now is 80 pages of fine print. That's why I'm here — to pull out what matters for your specific situation. What question do you need answered before we move forward?"*

**Pillar:** The Shift — enrollment is the safe move; you are the guide, not the brochure

---

### OBJECTION 3 — "My son / daughter already looked into this and said my plan is fine."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — family-authority stall

**What's underneath:** This is a stall with a third party as the cover story. The client isn't saying the plan is actually fine — they're saying someone they trust told them not to worry about it. The family member almost certainly didn't do a side-by-side comparison using current plan data.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"Well, things may have changed since they looked."* — sounds dismissive; they defend the family member
- Continuing to pitch as if the third party didn't exist — you lose both the client and the relationship

**✅ Option A:**
> *"I love that they're looking out for you — that's exactly the right instinct. Here's what I want to know: when they looked, did they do a side-by-side comparison of what you're getting now versus what's available in your area this year? Because plans change every year — what was true last fall may not be true today. I can do that comparison right now, live, in 2 minutes. If their answer holds up, I'll tell you and we're done."*

**✅ Option B — if they seem close to the third party decision:**
> *"Is there any chance we could get [son/daughter] on the phone right now? I'd love to walk both of you through the comparison at the same time — that way they hear the numbers directly and you don't have to relay anything."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — honor the family member; challenge the comparison, not the person

## TYPE 6 — THE TIME BOMB

### Who This Is
Willing and interested — but operating under a constraint that will end the call before enrollment if the agent doesn't move with urgency. A ride arriving. Exhaustion. Physical pain. Another appointment. A family member waiting. The window is real and it is closing. The agent who spends this call building rapport loses it.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"I don't have long — I have an appointment at 2."*
- *"My ride's going to be here soon."*
- *"I'm in a lot of pain today."*
- *"Can we make this quick?"*
- Client's energy drops noticeably during the call — fatigue, pain, or distraction setting in.

### Primary Move
Identify the constraint early. Name it. Accelerate. Move straight to what matters. Discovery can happen fast — skip everything that isn't essential to the close.

> *"I hear you — you've got limited time. Let me get straight to what matters. Tell me who you see for [primary condition] and I'll have everything pulled up in under a minute."*

**What to cut when the clock is short:** Skip the full discovery sequence. Get one doctor name, run the network check, state the benefit, annualize it, and close. The entire compressed sequence — from doctor name to close — should take 4 minutes or less. Rapport happens after the enrollment, not before. If you don't have the enrollment by the time their constraint hits, you don't have the enrollment.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "My ride's here / I have to go."

**Signal:** 🟡 YELLOW → hard stop coming

**What's underneath:** The window is closing. This is not an objection — it is a logistics fact. Your response determines whether you get the enrollment in the next 90 seconds or lose it entirely.

**✅ Option A — if close is near:**
> *"I hear you — let me get this wrapped up in the next 2 minutes. You've already agreed the math makes sense. All I need is [next step]. Let's get you set up before you go."*

**✅ Option B — if you're mid-call with no close yet:**
> *"Before you go — is there any chance we finish this when you get back? I'll hold everything I've pulled up. This saves you starting over. What time works?"*

Then call back at exactly that time and open with what you already found — not a new call.

**Pillar:** Refocusing — validate the constraint; accelerate or schedule with precision

---

### OBJECTION 2 — "I'm tired / I'm in pain. I need to think more clearly."

**Signal:** 🟡 YELLOW turning 🔴 RED

**What's underneath:** Physical state is the barrier, not the plan. Pushing through this loses the call and the relationship.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"I completely understand — and I don't want to make this harder on you. Here's where we are: I've already confirmed your doctor is covered and the benefit comes out to $[amount] a year. When you're feeling better, we can finish this in under 5 minutes because the work is already done. What time tomorrow would be best?"*

Do not restart from zero on the callback. Open with: *"I've got everything pulled up from when we spoke. Your doctor [name] is in-network and the plan pays $[amount]. Let's pick up where we left off."*

**Pillar:** Refocusing — remove the pressure; protect the relationship; keep the work done

## TYPE 7 — THE COMMERCIAL MYTH CALLER

### Who This Is
Their expectations were set by television advertising. They called because a commercial promised $6,000, $900, or some other number that doesn't match reality for their zip code or plan options. They are not resistant — they are confused and feel deceived. The wrong response makes the agent part of the betrayal.

### Watch For: Type 7 Is Often a Type 1 That Got Burned First
Type 7 is not a different kind of person from Type 1 — it's what happens to a Type 1 after the commercial set an expectation reality can't keep. The caller is the same motivated person. The difference is they arrived disappointed before you said a word. Type 1 is who they are. Type 7 is what happens when a Type 1 has already been burned by the commercial before you picked up. Once you defuse the frustration and anchor them on what's real, you run the same playbook as Type 1 — full Math Breakdown.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"The commercial said I could get $900 a month."*
- *"I saw on TV that Medicare pays you money."*
- *"They advertised a $6,000 benefit — where is that?"*
- *"The man on TV said to call this number for the $1,200 check."*
- *"Why is what you're offering so much less than what the commercial showed?"*

### Primary Move
Join their frustration before presenting anything. Become the ally against the misleading commercial — not the defender of the system. Then redirect toward what's real and available right now in their area.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "The commercial showed $6,000 / $900 / $1,200. Why is yours less?"

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — expectation mismatch and betrayal

**What's underneath:** They feel lied to. They need someone to validate that feeling — and then give them something real.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"I don't control the commercial."* — abandons all authority; you are now nobody on this call
- *"That's not how it works."* — shames them for believing it; call ends
- Defending the system in any way — you are now part of what betrayed them

**✅ Option A:**
> *"You're right to be frustrated — and I want to be straight with you. Those commercials show the national maximum, which is the highest benefit available anywhere in the country. Your actual benefit is based on your specific zip code and the plans available there. What I'm looking at for your area right now is $[amount] — and that's real. That's what hits your account every month starting [date]. The commercial showed the ceiling. I'm showing you what's actually on the table for you."*

**✅ Option B — pivot to total value:**
> *"I completely understand — those commercials are built to get you to call, and they leave out the most important part: your zip code determines your benefit. Here's what I want to show you instead: when you add up the grocery card, the Part B giveback, and what you're saving on deductibles, the real total for your situation is [annualized number]. That's the actual picture."*

**Pillar:** Reframing — validate the frustration; anchor on real available value

---

### OBJECTION 2 — "The commercial said A and B is all I need to get the card."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — deceived by advertising

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"I completely understand — those commercials make it sound automatic. What they don't explain is that the government distributes those funds through specific plans. You've already done the hard part by getting Parts A and B. My job is to make sure that money actually gets to you instead of sitting unclaimed. Can I check what you qualify for right now?"*

**Pillar:** Reframing — become the ally; position enrollment as the final step the commercial left out

## TYPE 8 — THE VETERAN

### Who This Is
They use VA benefits and see Medicare Advantage as a threat to what they've earned. They believe enrolling in a Medicare plan means replacing their VA care, losing their VA benefits, or making a choice that dishonors their service. None of that is true — but they don't know it yet. And if the agent jumps straight to plan mechanics without addressing this fear, the call ends.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"I get my care through the VA — I don't need this."*
- *"I'm a veteran. VA covers everything."*
- *"I already have VA benefits. Why would I need Medicare?"*
- Client hesitates or shuts down the moment PCP selection comes up.
- *"I'm not picking a different doctor. My VA doctor is my doctor."*

### Primary Move
Neutralize the displacement fear before presenting anything. VA and Medicare Advantage are additive, not competing. Establish this as the first thing they hear.

> *"Your VA covers everything at the VA. This covers everything outside the VA. They work together — this is additive, not a replacement. You're not choosing one over the other."*

Do not move to PCP selection, plan details, or math until this is understood and accepted.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "I get my care at the VA. I don't need Medicare."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — displacement fear

**What's underneath:** They believe enrolling replaces or threatens VA coverage. This is the wall. Everything else waits.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- Jumping to PCP selection — the moment a veteran hears "pick a primary care doctor," they hear "replace your VA doctor"
- Explaining plan mechanics before addressing the fear — they stop listening the moment the fear isn't acknowledged

**✅ Option A:**
> *"Your VA care stays exactly as it is — nothing about this changes that. What Medicare Advantage covers is everything outside the VA: urgent care when you're traveling, specialists in the community, dental, OTC benefits, and emergency situations where getting to the VA isn't realistic. Think of it as a second layer that works alongside your VA coverage — not instead of it."*

**✅ Option B — connecting to real cost:**
> *"Here's the gap I want to show you: your VA covers you at VA facilities. Outside of that, you're exposed. An ER visit, a specialist your VA can't get you into fast enough, a procedure that requires a community provider — those costs land on you right now. This plan covers that gap at $0 premium. Your VA doesn't change. You just stop being exposed."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — VA and Medicare are complementary; add coverage, don't replace it

---

### OBJECTION 2 — "I'm not picking a primary care doctor. My VA doctor is mine."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — PCP resistance

**What's underneath:** The PCP requirement feels like being forced to abandon their VA provider. It is an administrative requirement, not a clinical replacement.

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"I hear you — and your VA doctor stays your VA doctor. The PCP field in the enrollment is an administrative requirement for the system. It doesn't mean you're changing who you see. Your VA care is completely separate from this plan. Think of the PCP as a placeholder for the plan's records — it doesn't replace Dr. [VA doctor]. Would it help to pick someone close to you as a backup for non-VA situations?"*

**Pillar:** Reframing — the PCP field is administrative; VA care is untouched

---

## TYPE 9 — THE TIMING OBJECTOR

### Who This Is
Willing and interested — but fixated on a timing issue that feels logical and is almost always a stall. Effective date is too far away. They have an appointment this week. They're moving in six months. They want to wait for Medicaid. They want to talk it over at the holidays. The stall sounds reasonable. The cost of waiting is real — and they don't know it yet.

### How to Identify in the First 2 Minutes
- *"Can this start sooner? I have a procedure next week."*
- *"I'm moving in a few months — let me wait until I get settled."*
- *"I want to wait and see if I get Medicaid."*
- *"Let me get through the holidays first."*
- *"I'll do this next year during open enrollment."*

### Primary Move
Make the wait feel expensive in specific dollars. Every month of waiting is a number. Say it. Then make enrollment the low-risk option so there's no reason to wait.

---

### OBJECTION 1 — "I have an appointment next week. I don't want to mess up my coverage."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — fear of a coverage gap

**What's underneath:** Fear that switching will leave them without coverage at a critical moment. This is legitimate. Resolve it directly.

**✅ Option A:**
> *"Completely valid concern — and here's the important thing: your current coverage stays active until the new plan's effective date. There is no gap. Your appointment next week is fully covered under your current plan. Once the new plan starts, [benefit] starts too. You're protected the whole way through."*

**✅ Option B — when effective date is the concern:**
> *"I hear you — let's look at what date we can make this effective. If we enroll today, the soonest start date would be [date]. That's after your appointment. You have full coverage for that visit, and then the new plan — with [benefit] — kicks in right after."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — no gap exists; neutralize the fear with facts

---

### OBJECTION 2 — "I'm moving soon. Let me wait until I'm settled."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — timing stall

**What's underneath:** The move feels like a reason to pause everything. It is usually not. Plans are available in most areas and the SEP protects a move. The cost of waiting is real.

**✅ Option A — take control, check the destination:**
> *"Where are you moving? Let me check right now what plans are available there — that way you're making a decision based on what you're actually moving to, not a guess. This takes 2 minutes."*

**✅ Option B — if destination is unknown or far off:**
> *"Here's what I want you to know: if you move to a new area, there's a Special Enrollment Period that lets you change your plan at that time. So you don't have to choose between waiting and acting. We can enroll you now, you get [benefit] starting [date], and when you move, you have the right to adjust your plan based on what's available there. You're not locked in — you're covered."*

**Pillar:** The Shift — SEP protects the move; make the wait cost them money

---

### OBJECTION 3 — "I'll do this next open enrollment."

**Signal:** 🔴 RED — delay stall

**What's underneath:** They believe waiting is safe. It is not free. Every month of waiting is a specific dollar amount in unclaimed benefits.

**❌ What NOT to say:**
- *"OK, I'll call you back in the fall."* — this call and this client are gone

**✅ Correct Response:**
> *"I hear you — and I want to show you what that wait actually costs. The benefit we're talking about is $[monthly]. Between now and next open enrollment is [X] months. That's $[X × monthly] you won't get back. That money is already funded — it exists right now for people in your zip code. The only question is whether it goes to you or someone else. What would you need to see to feel comfortable moving forward today?"*

**Pillar:** The Shift — the wait has a specific dollar cost; make it visible

## CLOSE CONFIRMATION — NON-NEGOTIABLE ON EVERY CALL

After enrollment is submitted — before you hang up — re-anchor the client's confidence. This takes 20–30 seconds. It is the difference between a retained enrollment and a disenrollment.

Remind them of the specific benefit that moved them. Connect it to something they personally told you during the call. Tell them they made the right call — with conviction. Give them your direct number.

> *"Before I let you go — starting [date], [specific benefit] is yours. That's [annual amount] every year, back in your pocket. You made the right call today. If anything comes up between now and then, call me directly."*

**Why this matters:** A client who hangs up uncertain is a disenrollment in slow motion. The enrollment is not the finish line. The close confirmation is.
